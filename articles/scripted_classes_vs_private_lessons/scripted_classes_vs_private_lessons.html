Scripted Classes vs Private Lessons

A complete and thorough discussion on this topic likely demands and deserves a book, or many books, but I hope can convey some insights throughout this article.

Scripted classes and group classes are definitely not the same, but I'll use them interchangeably in this article. That's mostly because private lessons, in contrast, when done well, are not scripted, and obviously not in group.

The numbering below does not reflect any hierarchy of values, it's just for reference purposes:

  1. The current situation of the educational system is understandable but not acceptable.
    • It's 2021 and we are still stuck with the same educational system and logic from more than about 200 years ago. Everyone complains, everyone criticizes it, and we still can't seem to move on.
    • Every ounce of effort spent trying to change the current system from within might backfire into preserving it. I'm not saying it will, but how many people have offered valuable changes to the current system only for it to still stay mostly the same as it was more than a hundred years ago?
    • My suggestion is simple: lean more heavily on using technologies to help diminish the amount of repetitive teaching, while saving the teacher's time to use in private lessons and invididual consulting.
  2. The ability to record lectures diminishes the need for this repetitive task, which frees up the teacher's time, leaving more time for private, targeted tutoring.
    • Up to maybe the 1950s, this was not possible, but we have come a long way now. The internet now grants us access to content from around the world, not only what can be shipped; and software gives us many more functionalities, like forums, commenting, interactive problem solving, etc.
  3. Motivation has always been one of the major tasks of teaching. Mandatory lectures mask this, obfuscating the problem.
    • One of the major criticisms about leaning heavily on the recording lectures methodology is that the teacher now has to enforce students to watch the lectures, because, if they don't, then she will need to answer repetitive questions individually again and again. But allow me to reframe this objection: aren't students forced to watch lectures in the current system anyway? And isn't one of the most important aspects of teaching simply instilling motivation in students?
    • Just like when discussing cryptocurrencies, every critique of this new approach seems to be, in reality, a critique of the current system. The lack of motivation in current students isn't inherent to students, it's just a problem the current system fails to address. More passive, scripted lectures, and more mandatory subjects won't make the situation any better; in fact, anyone with the slightest of experience in this system will agree it will likely only make it worse .
  4. Scripted or group lectures do not offer almost any value when it comes social interactions. Most social interactions derived from them happen afterwards, which could happen with recorded lectures anyway.
    • But what about the social aspect of being part of lectures? Well, that's as much bad logic as going to the cinemas for dating: you're not having almost any social interaction when all parties are individually focused. Most of the social aspect people remember coming from lectures actually come from after them, and that same conversation or interaction could happen if you had seen the lecture in video form, asynchronously. I do agree, though, that the social bond of live lectures, even online, tends to be much stronger than other options, but I don't believe it justifies its existence as the default mode of learning, or outweighs the negatives.
  5. The best professionals almost all had great close instruction and coaching.
    • To me, that's even a no-brainer when you analyze the best professionals in the market. Most of them have had great advisors or bosses in order to get to the top: master artisans don't learn everything from the ether. Even Mozart, likely the greatest musical genius, had the help of father, one of the greatest violinist of his time.
  6. No amount of lecturing will ever address all individual perspectives and perceptions of a subject.
    • Paraphrasing it, that means we cannot address all the possible interpretations students will get from having contact with a subject, which then requires either the teacher or the student to look for the blind spots and help with lingering questions.
  7. The current trend of teaching methodologies is just another form of lecturing, which offer considerable diminishing returns when compared to private lessons.
    • And no, I'm not advocating for the total extinction of group lecturing, I'm just arguing that it should be a much rarer occurrence than it is now, its benefits are diminishing everyday, with new technologies.
    • I hope this psychological insufficiency also puts a last nail on the methodologies coffin. Yes, there are better techniques for teaching — which vary according to different subjects, by the way —, and the last 2 centuries' methodologies have improved them a lot, however the diminishing returns on them don't justify at all their current importance and emphasis. There's a reason why private teaching is still the most primal and the most effective way of teaching.
  8. Theory isn't enough, practice and constant feedback is what builds expertise.
    • Hasn't the pandemic proven that overemphasis on theory isn't enough to be able to solve a problem? Researchers argued for decades that gain of function research would help us prepare for pandemics, but it turns out that the risks outweighed the negaives. That's when theory and argumentation took over reality and experimentation. It's a lengthy topic, but it's clear that researchers' vanity and corruption — e.g. Peter Daszak and Ian Lipkin — had a huge influence in this last pandemic.
  9. Scripted classes are time-wise practical for the teacher but time-wise impractical for the student.
  10. Scripted classes create bad habits: scripted answers.
    • And they might also be at the origin of the test epidemic in our educational systems.
  11. Private lessons help avoid shame when making mistakes.
  12. Recorded classes and private lessons do not diminish the students' ability of posing questions.
    • That's because current online learning software lets students pose questions anyway.
    • And software will even lower the burden on the teacher: no more need for always answering the same questions.
  13. Private lessons enable more targeted and motivated homework.
    • The student can ask to work on related projects with hopefully real interest, instead of the typical generic homework which do not appeal to no one.
