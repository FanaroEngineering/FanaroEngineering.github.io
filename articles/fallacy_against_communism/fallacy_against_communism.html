Why Your Main Argument Against Socialism/Communism Is Wrong (Probably)

For probably about 10 years I've been hearing this fallacy, and I cannot believe it sometimes comes out from some quite erudite people; lately I've been hearing it even from more intellectual Youtube channels such as China Uncensored — of which I'm a subscriber and fan. The typical phrasing is something like "that's what you get for choosing communism: only death" or "why would you choose socialism? Look at the hundred million deaths all of these regimes have caused...".

Okay, okay... you're gonna say that, as statements of facts, those are more or less correct, because communism or socialism did kill a lot of people. However, whenever people invoke those arguments, the comparison to the status quo, i.e. some variation of capitalism, is definitely implicit and, as we will see, that does lead to false conclusions.

I usually don't even know where to start with this kind of reasoning and end up sounding like some sort of anti-capitalist or flat out strike organizer. But, before you think I'm a staunch supporter of socialism or brutal dictatorships, let me tell you that I'm not: I'm, at best, a fan of logic. And that's the core problem here: sponsoring this fallacy is playing — often unconsciously — on emotions to sell the propaganda of an economic system, not analyzing the facts.

Pressured by external and internal forces, the government would be forced into taking more and more extreme actions, killing many of its citizens in the name of the revolution and implementing an atmosfere of extreme distrust and censorship.

To which revolution am I referring?

Usually people blatantly tag socialism or communism as something inherently wrong and, thus, bound to fail. However, it is only an ideology, a plethora of ideas, and these ideas are only as extreme as the ideal capitalism some hypocritical economists seem to propagate as reality. A perfectly autruistic society probably never existed (for long), and there will probably never be a perfectly balanced market either, so long as there are cheaters in this world or so long as we let businesses influence politics.

These plethoras of ideas need to face reality at some point and that's how they fail; and fail hard sometimes. For example, we haven't yet been able to even conceive a market where the government would not have to correct the course in which companies regularly abuse their power to the detriment of the people.

Mostly, these implementation failures come from the ideas' neglect of many other important variables. In the case of communism, the biggest variable theories underestimated was corruption: the infamous politburos would not, in the end, fight for the people, but rather for their own interests, with infinite attempts at perpetuating their own power.

Despite this not so mentioned implementation flaw, there is another huge variable shallow analysts and the biased media seem to ignore frequently: outside influence. Can anyone imagine the pressure the USSR must have faced by going against the entire world and institutionalizing a completely different system? Every day there would be numerous attempts at destabilizing the government, and from completely different countries. No wonder the KGB quickly became the most powerful secret agency.

Little by little, the capitalist world chipped away the allure of socialism — through words or products — from the Russians, Slavs, Eastern Germans, and others. Little by little did the USSR increase their surveillance and punishment to those who spoke ill of the system.

To prove that this phenomenon is not an exception, a very specific instance or something else entirely, let's take a look at an "entirely different situation": the French Revolution of 1789. An analogous pressure was felt among the rebels with respect to the outside influence: all over Europe, monarchies would soon flock to the rescue of French royalty, fearing the spread of such a rebellion. The disgraced royalty would try to gather forces in order to undo the coup d'état. Little by little, an atmosfere of distrust ensued and the rebels had to gradually and quickly take more and more severe actions against anyone who would dare doubt them. Throughout the continent, from various countries, powerful politicians would declare the revolution an abomination, a new system that usurped the glory nobility perpetuated. Sounds familiar? If you're still rooting for the main fallacy: your cries against socialism could have sounded as strange to future humanity, as the nobles' cries against democracy and capitalism back then.

Only 5 years after the revolution, Robespierre, one of its most renowned leaders, was executed by the guillotine. About a decade after the start of the whole chaotic ordeal, Napoléon Bonaparte would be crowned the Emperor of France. Somehow, it should seem miraculous that most of the world lives under democracies and not monarchies. Somehow, we have convinced ourselves that the exact same phenomenon was brave in the context of the French Revolution and hideous, logically invalid in the context of the USSR.

Both contexts are different and I'm not here to defend the Stalin's brutalities on the back of the French Revolution. But it should be flagrant that people seem to inflate brutalities when the outcome is bad and deflate them when things turned out to be successful.

If deaths could comprise the hardship and complexities of living under economic systems, we wouldn't really need a debate, we would just (carefully) count them and game over. But deaths are just a huge oversimplification; and, even though I do agree that communism has really gone to extremes never before seen during Stalin and Mao, it would be very naïve or ill-intentioned to ignore the terrible background capitalist feats when it comes to the quality of living.

It is true that the current system has managed to improve our lives beyond our wildest imaginations — if you have the money that is... —, however this was not simply only an achievement of will or intellectual prowess on the part of the most developed countries, it was mostly built on the backs of the millions and billions living in the poorest countries. In the last century, the rich bathed in comfort while poverty was exported to the far away corners of the world, in Southeast Asia, Africa and South America.

The most notable case of hypocrisy is probably the abusive British colonial rule of India. The English would regularly exploit the country for food, causing huge famines in various regions and in different periods. The current estimate of the number of deaths is at 35 million people. This no small number, it's doubling Hitler's death toll and 1.5 times Stalin's hideous achievement. And the English didn't stop there: during World War II, they forcefully recruited millions 2.5 million Indian soldiers and, while in shortage of food for their army, caused 4 million people to die of famine (again). The British still sneakily try to diverge from having these facts publicized to the world and, in the meanwhile, let Hollywood make endless movies about American heroism in a war where only 1 out of 50 soldiers killed was actually American — if we include civilians, only 1 in 200 was American.

Famine or no famine, Indians will breed like rabbits.

Winston Churchill

Winston Churchill was even worse than you imagine when it comes to Indians. After refusing American and Canadian food aid to India and being asked why — food supplies to Europe had already been sent —, he replied: "if there is a shortage then why is Gandhi still alive?"

History will be kind to me, for I intend to write it.

Winston Churchill

Another less known brutal ruler was Leopold II of Belgium. During the colonial European rush of the late 1800s, Belgium managed to get its hands on Congo, where a brutal system was imposed, leading to internal wars and, again, huge famines. In total, it's estimated that Belgium has managed to destroy up to 15 million lives — mutilation of children included — as a result of either direct actions or the sponsorship of internal conflicts.

To further illustrate that deaths as a metric system is completely incompetent for the argument, we can also mention the South American dictatorships dating from the 1960s to the early 1990s. While trying to fight USSR's influence in the continent after the Cuban disaster, the USA undercut its own principles and sponsored several coup d'états all over the continent, installing brutal dictators who would perpetuate the exact same methods of torture, censorship and killing the USSR was known for. The death tolls were not in the same magnitude of prior atrocities, but what about the freedoms lost? The shattered families? The brainwashing of the people? These regimes weren't even capable to stand for themselves, leaving their countries with frail or completely destroyed economies. Once the US decided to withdraw their aid, those governments collapsed.

The main reason people can't seem to get their numbers right is related to what was discussed in the previous section: only deaths caused by conflicts approved by the status quo seem to make the bill.

If you sum the deaths caused by iconic communist dictators, the total is probably around 100 to 120 million, and that would even be called a bit of an overestimation by some.

On the other hand, when summing up only some of the major conflicts capitalism has caused throughout more or less the last 150 years, the total can easily surpass the socialist counterpart:

  • 40 million: World War I
  • 55 million: World War II. Should I mention the fact that both World Wars were mainly caused by capitalist strife between Europeans? Or that many huge US companies helped the Nazis, only stopping when the government forced them to?
  • 10 million: Leopold II of Belgium's rule of Congo (I'm trying to be a bit more conservative than 15 million)
  • 35 million: Indians lost by famine during British rule
  • 4 million: Indians lost by famine due to increased British food demand in World War II

The list is only a brief summary of some conflicts and we are already at 144 million deaths for capitalism. I've even ignored plenty of other conflicts in the Middle East, the abominable European economic wonders slavery provided, and all of the nastiness the Japanese Empire (1868 - 1947) caused Southeast Asia from the beginning of the 1900s to 1947. It's also not at all difficult to imagine another 100 million being added to the amount to account for the generalized famine in Africa during the last century.

People should care very little if you don't like socialism, communism or capitalism, they are only choices on the spectrum. Ultimately, all of those systems have worthy goals, worthy ideas, despite the implementations' faults. Nevertheless, what you should definitely care about is if the reason for the dislike comes from emotion or propaganda, instead of logic. The moment you fall into the trap, it won't be you or your reasoning who's in control, but rather the (powerful) people who want to perpetuate the(ir) status quo.

In sum, all I have to ask is: if you wish to continue arguing against socialism or communism, be my guest, but do it with another argument, a logical one this time.
