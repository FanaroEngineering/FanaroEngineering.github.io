Reenter the Dragon: Engineering

In a sunny day, a year and a half ago, I was finally receiving my bachelor’s diploma, after six years, including one abroad, of a very long battle. What should have been a happy day turned out to be only the realization of my spirit’s depletion. University had absolutely crushed my enthusiasm. Frustrated by my lack of energy, I resorted to basically the only pleasure I had left: Go. Some months later I would go to South Korea to hone my skills and dedicate my whole being to the sport that gave me the joy I lacked while enduring the mostly bashing my head against the wall my engineering course was.

Why? This whole time I was trying to understand the shitstorm those years were and I won’t claim I have all the answers, I’m not that wise, but some of the roots of the problem are quite clear to me now.

I believe the main reason of my torment was the teaching methodology — along with the basically total lack of guidance from the professors — prioritized by universities. For some reason, still in the 21st century, we still are victims of the bottom-up consensus, where the student, having no idea of what is going on, is forced to understand complicated concepts, in the hopes that someday an angelic comprehension of the whole might arrive, saving the day and, much more importantly, his or her confidence. Let me emphasize this last part: confidence. Let me repeat it to you: confidence. This is not a hyperbolic catchy statement to justify tautologically the phenomenon: it’s science.

Studies in psychology attest the direct relationship between confidence and reasoning. Strained confidence invariably lowers the individual’s IQ, and the self-realization of the diminished capabilities triggers the feedback loop of a trap that sometimes may even become depression. That’s why, in interviews for companies with stressful environments, the interviewer usually attacks personal achievements and, only then, asks test’s questions.

The relationship of the confidence loop with the bottom-up approach is key to understanding the failure of many, if not most, universities. Due to the uncertainty of not knowing the logical connections between the parts — i.e., the big picture — of what’s being taught, the student will always question if what he knows is not a mistake, a big and frequent blow to confidence.

In the book Microsociology , by Thomas J. Scheff, what happens in the long run is discussed, and the author offers, from the literature, two solutions to break the loop, aside from a non-humiliating mentorship. The first one is, based on studies of numerous remarkable artists and philosophers — e.g., Nietzsche, Wagner and Freud —, the path to genius: laughter. Statistically, it was verified that the one true denominator among most of the geniuses is self-derision, a way of dispelling the negative effect of the bad loop by diminishing its value. However, more importantly, given it is much more common, the alternative solution might be even sadder than being a victim of the phenomenon: indifference. And that’s the epidemic which took over most of my colleagues. To defend oneself from the attacks of our course’s curriculum with the shield of indifference has become the norm for today’s students, which imperatively means the death of all the enthusiasm that brought them to the course in the first place. As a result, most of my peers would not even care about diverging from an engineer’s career, after all, at that point, it made no difference.

But wait, there’s more. If you think the problems with bottom-upping end with the hideous confidence trap, you’ll be surprised. I will only cite here one of the other problems: the disparity between life in university and in the real world. And I don’t mean couchsurfing and partying regularly. The actual problem is that the courses, especially in exact sciences, focus all of their efforts in a firehose stream of bickering about details: there is too small an emphasis on the building of intuition, the bread and butter of everyday activities. In universities, you will find yourself for days embroiled in endless derivations that I assure you, unless you become a researcher, will mostly be useless. While the intent of the professor with them is noble, not giving you guidance — some teachers do give you guidance, of course; while the vast majority usually provides either cryptic advice or statements with poor connections to the whole — of what exactly to focus on is a waste of your time and, also, his expertise.

At this point, I hope I have convinced you that I had some reasons — perhaps I have been too extreme? — to distance myself from this field which distilled me of all my prior passion for exact sciences, left me with endless existential momentaneous insecurities and, in exchange, gave me a diploma. Lastly, with true sadness, I can’t help but downgrade most of the professors I’ve met as the worst type of tutor, the hypocrite kind, one that sees the pain of learning under such a centuries outdated horrible algorithm and, still, blames it on the student.

In truth, this sabbatical period has been a rehab for me. I am finally close to fully disintoxicated, and soon I’ll be able to have a point of view less biased by my frustrations; and, hopefully, I’ll get a place at the field that most interests me right now: machine learning.

In this last year and a half, I would say I had two major breakthroughs that really broadened my perspective on life and which I would like to briefly discuss here. The first one was my stay in South Korea and a trip to China.

Naively, I went there thinking I would not have too much trouble trying to adapt, in part because I had already lived abroad in the past; and, also, due to globalization: I believed their habits, at this point in time, would be not so out of line with ours, westerners. Unsurprisingly, I was deeply wrong: the depth of the cultural difference between us is still abysmal. Everyday I was aghast at how they would mostly just follow the flow, repeat tasks and put their intuition above all other methods of achieving the same results — e.g. logic, the one I had invested most of my life on. That way of living caused a huge impact on me and, slowly, I am privileging intuition more and more over formal logical reasoning. We all make mistakes and we should use them to hone our intuition and not as a justification to question our knowledge at every step — curiously, that’s also the lesson machine learning is teaching us.

The second turning point, perhaps even more crucial, has been studying for jobs in the government, which are obtained through open exams here in Brazil. The core of the change is in thinking about how to study, not only about what to study. A better method yields more efficiency, but, as you’re using the optimal solution, it also generates a significant boost to your confidence. This has been a deciding change for me; even though most of my praxis hasn’t changed, I am now much more confident, since I know that what I do is close to best.

In the last two weeks, I’ve noticed a big — and somewhat involuntary — change in my interests: I can’t even focus properly on my upcoming exam anymore! I’ve been frantically scooping the internet for everything related to machine learning, I’ve watched a lot of lectures, read many excerpts from various books and even coded some algorithms. All with the top-bottom approach of first doing and then learning about the details. These last days have been a revolution in my life — one I hope, and feel, will last —, and I am finally, after years of flat out disinterest for life, enthusiastic about something again. Hooray! Cheers to the future!
