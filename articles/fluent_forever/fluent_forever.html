Fluent Forever: The Book I Wish I Had Read Years Ago

Edit from the future: if you're really interested in this topic, you might be interested in the The Loom of Language seminal book, by Frederick Bodmer and Lancelot Hogben. That's the "secret" book linguists use to learn languages as if they were on steroids, though there are more recent spin-offs of that masterpiece.

Fluent Forever was written by Gabriel Wyner, an engineer turned opera singer who, after long years of struggle trying to learn languages, found himself in the urgent situation of having to quickly learn multiple of them in order to enter the world of professional opera singers. Gabriel tells a story very similar to my experience with German, where he, for years, had Russian lessons that simply used rote memorization, and which, inevitably, yielded tragic results: not much beyond the alphabet and some common phrases and nouns were learned.

Years later, he had to learn German, but this time it had to be with a different approach. It wasn't as if he knew what he was doing but this experience would shine a light in his head about how to become fluent in any language. During weeks he went into an immersion program in the famous Middlebury College where he could only speak the foreign language he had signed up for &mdash otherwise he would be expelled from the campus until the end of the program. Although he didn't stress it much, that period would serve to illustrate his principle of avoiding translations when learning a new language, even if you don't understand anything of what you're hearing. He argues that, at first, you should learn the blunt sounds of the language, because not only is it easier to learn a new language when remarkable sounds come into play &mdash they help memories become more memorable &mdash, but they also help you disconnect the crutches of bridging everything you learn into your native language. This disconnection will free your thoughts and maximize learning efficiency. Of course, thankfully, he doesn't go into fascist mode and says we should abandon translation absolutely, only that we should avoid it whenever possible.

The second principle that underpins his whole book is probably even more important and less intuitive to discover how to apply: make memories more memorable. Each grammatical construction, word or phrase is a memory in your head; and, thus, the least attachment, personal connection, they have with you, the less likely they will go out of the short memory buffer and enter the long memory hard-drive. He cites a lot of research he has gathered over the years in order to make this point &mdash which is probably the most crucial of the book &mdash, and, in the end, the simple solution to end our memorization problems is to come up with a systematic way of creating effective memories. In his experience, the best way to do so is to use Flash Cards &mdash software like Anki, or, equivalently, a Leitner Box, help optimize this process &mdash to stimulate recalling sessions of the memories, something that will work best only if you add something memorable to each card &mdash for example, in order to remember the word "fire", you might put a picture of the Great Fire of Rome caused by the ancient Roman Emperor Nero. Due to the importance of flash cards, he probably spends 30-40% of the book on techniques to optimize their use, which, albeit very important indeed, does become a bit mechanical and uninteresting after a while, especially if you're not practicing what he teaches as you read.

In order to drive his point about memorization with Spaced Repetition Systems (of which flash cards is an example) home, he cites a lot of research, but the main one comes from the German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus. Ebbinghaus became famous after painstakingly memorizing nonsensical syllables (dax, bok, yat, etc...) in order to create the graph now known as the Forgetting Curve. This curve shows how much content we can retain in our memory after a given time since we've learned it. Spaced Repetition Systems (SRS) base themselves on this curve: whenever the content retained reaches a certain level, we recall our memories via some registry &mdash e.g. flash cards &mdash and reset our memories until the content finally reaches our long-term memory, something that is illustrated in the picture below.

All in all, I would give this book 4.5 stars due to the excessive unnecessary sections on flash cards; it seemed the author wanted the book to be longer for some reason. Since 4.5 stars is usually not possible in most websites, I'm gonna give it some slack and round it up to 5 stars. It's a great book, an absolute must for anyone trying to be more efficient when learning a new language (or simply memorizing stuff); and I also would really like to investigate the interesting references more.
